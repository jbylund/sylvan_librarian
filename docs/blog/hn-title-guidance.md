# HN Title and Framing Guidance

Derived from analyzing ~70 top Hacker News front-page posts across four dates (April–June 2026).
Use this to review blog post titles and framing before publishing.

## The core pattern

Every top post combines a **specific, verifiable fact** with either an **unexpected outcome** or a **definitive stance**. The title is a claim, not a category description. Vague titles, tutorial-style "how to" titles, and listicles are almost entirely absent from the top 10 on any day.

## 9 rules

**1. Put the measurement in the title, not the body.**
If you ran a benchmark, the headline number belongs in the title. "Building an HTML-first site doubled our users overnight" works. "How we improved performance" does not. The measurement is the claim — without it there is no reason to click.

**2. Use the "X did Y [unexpected outcome]" structure when you have a result.**
Subject → verb → outcome. Put the most surprising fact last. "Claude Desktop spawns 1.8 GB Hyper-V VM on every launch, even for chat-only use." The last clause is where the surprise lands.

**3. Start with "Stop" or "Goodbye" when recommending abandonment of something widely used.**
"Stop Using JWTs", "Goodbye Visa and Mastercard". This communicates your conclusion before the reader finishes the title and self-selects for readers who are already skeptical. Do not soften to "Reconsidering JWTs" — that reads as a hedge.

**4. Include the age or duration for legacy/archaeology posts.**
"Fixing a 20-year-old bug in Enlightenment E16" — the "20-year-old" signals the depth of the dig and is the punchline. The duration is the hook.

**5. For "I built X" posts, name the discovery or limitation, not the feature.**
"Show HN: Forge – Guardrails take an 8B model from 53% to 99%" succeeds because it names the before/after metric. "I hacked into the worst e-bike and fixed it" works because "the worst" signals a story. Feature descriptions do not.

**6. Use "TIL" or "I [verb]ed" for genuine peer-level discoveries.**
"TIL: You can make HTTP requests without curl using Bash /dev/TCP" — the frame signals genuine surprise and positions the writer as sharing a discovery, not instructing. Gets "I didn't know that either" upvotes.

**7. Ask a question only when the answer is genuinely divisive among practitioners.**
"Do you even need a database?" and "Is Meta destroying its engineering organization?" both generated high comment counts because practitioners have strong, divergent answers. A question for beginners or one with an obvious answer gets no traction.

**8. Include the specific number for policy or company decision posts.**
"Anthropic requires 30 day data retention" — without the number it is a vague complaint; with it, it is a verifiable fact people can test against their own experience.

**9. Do not soften a contrarian claim.**
"CEOs who think AI replaces their employees are just bad CEOs" — flat and declarative. The hedged version ("Some reflections on AI workforce strategy") removes the reason to click. If you believe something, state it flat. The comments will supply the pushback.

## Points vs. comments

These are correlated but not tightly. The outliers are useful to understand:

- **High points, low comments** — delightful or impressive, nothing to argue about. People upvote and move on (infographics, visual demos, nostalgia).
- **High comments, modest points** — takes a definitive position half the audience will rebut. The post makes a claim someone feels compelled to respond to.

If you want reach (upvotes), show something beautiful or surprising and indisputable. If you want discussion, make a claim people disagree about.

## Titles that tend not to land

- Vague category descriptions: "Thoughts on X", "Reflections on Y", "Some notes on Z"
- Listicles with counts in the title: "5 ways to...", "10 things about..."
- Tutorial titles without a specific outcome: "How to configure X", "Getting started with Y"
- Hedged contrarian takes: "Why you might want to reconsider X"
